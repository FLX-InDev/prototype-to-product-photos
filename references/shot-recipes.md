# Product-use routing and French luxury shot recipes

Use the product description to choose supports and interactions. Never infer use from shape. Apply [product-rules.md](product-rules.md) before selecting a shot.

## Universal placement and integration

- Keep the product on or in a support explicitly allowed by the description.
- Repeat forbidden uses as hard negatives in every generation and retry.
- Keep a textile visibly removable from furniture unless it is explicitly upholstery.
- Preserve realistic scale; request dimensions when scale materially affects the scene.
- Keep props from a different category, smaller in visual importance, and physically separate.
- Preserve physically correct perspective, contact shadow, highlight direction, reflection, color temperature, brightness, saturation, depth of field, and edge sharpness.
- Reject floating contact, pasted-on edges, halos, inconsistent grain or blur, mismatched light direction, and global product recoloring.
- Reject any scene that makes the product appear to be another product class.

Default to 4:5 portrait unless the user specifies another ratio.

## Authentic French setting lanes

### Paris Haussmann apartment or hôtel particulier

Use tall ceilings and windows, restrained boiserie or panel moulding, aged herringbone or chevron oak parquet, limestone or marble fireplace details, pale lime plaster, substantial crafted furniture, and antique brass or sculptural lighting.

Keep the room collected and residential. Avoid excessive gilding, palace-scale ornament, Eiffel Tower references, café props, theatrical symmetry, and Paris clichés.

### South-of-France limestone villa or Provençal collector's home

Use limewashed stone, softly irregular plaster, pale or time-darkened oak, limestone, travertine, sheer linen curtains, deep-set windows, timber shutters, restrained terracotta, artisan furniture, and warm Mediterranean daylight.

Avoid nautical props, shells, anchors, generic blue-and-white styling, tropical décor, farmhouse clichés, and rustic clutter.

### Côte d’Azur modernist villa

Use rigorous modernist lines, sea-washed mineral color, travertine or terrazzo, warm timber, art-led furniture, refined glazing, linen window treatments, and one controlled coastal sightline.

Avoid generic white boxes, flashy yachts, resort branding, excessive chrome, nightclub lighting, and implausible ocean views.

### Refined wicker winter garden

Use high-quality wicker or cane furniture, stone or patterned tile, mature greenery, painted ironwork, architectural glazing, layered textiles, and soft directional daylight. Keep plants curated rather than jungle-like.

### Dark-wood private residence

Use dark oak or walnut joinery, hand-knotted rugs only as background décor when they cannot confuse the product category, sculptural lamps, collected books or art, rich but restrained upholstery, and controlled pools of light.

### Luxury Mediterranean vacation residence

Choose one coherent identity: Riviera hotel suite, Provençal mas, design-led coastal home, or private villa. Use hospitality-grade scale, bespoke joinery, natural materials, controlled sightlines, and one subtle destination cue.

Avoid generic hotel rooms, lobby styling, excessive marble, signage, amenities, fake luxury, and random views.

## Interior density requirement

Every medium or wide scene needs a coherent combination of authentic architecture, one substantial furniture anchor, layered natural finishes, refined lighting, intentional window treatment or soft furnishing, curated art or objects, and controlled negative space.

For detail images, retain at least three legible background cues that connect the frame to the same premium interior without competing with the product.

## Photography grammar

Choose grammar to reveal use and construction, not merely to decorate the scene.

### Low-angle three-quarter view

Use for a draped blanket, sculptural cushion group, vase, or furniture-supported product when height, silhouette, and material weight need emphasis. Keep verticals controlled and avoid heroic distortion.

### Slightly overhead window-side still life

Use for folded textiles, trays, stones, vases, eye masks, and scrunchies. Reveal edge topology, surface texture, and exact inventory. Keep the product plane believable and avoid flat-lay treatment when it could imply a forbidden floor use.

### Ground-level lounge composition

Use for blankets or cushions on approved lounge chairs, armchairs, daybeds, or wicker chairs. Show enough support structure to prove correct use. Do not let a blanket become a rug or upholstery.

### Frontal axial collector salon

Use for a formal product-truth lifestyle view in a Haussmann room or hôtel particulier. Maintain relaxed product styling within an architecturally disciplined composition.

### Dusk side-lit cinematic composition

Use warm practicals plus directional window or doorway light to reveal texture and room craftsmanship. Keep shadows soft enough for product verification and do not allow ambient color to recolor the product globally.

### Diagonal daybed composition

Use for bedding, blankets, and cushions when the long diagonal reveals layers, edge details, folds, and room depth. Keep bedding on the bed and all category boundaries clear.

## Shot types

### Product-truth image

- Use a simple approved support within a premium setting.
- Keep the product dominant, color-credible, construction-readable, and materially legible.
- Show component count, category, proportions, and defining edges clearly.

### Lifestyle-use image

- Show one approved interaction with a bed, sofa, chair, table, shelf, mantel, niche, or plinth.
- Make intended use immediately legible.
- Use controlled lived-in styling for soft goods without hiding construction evidence.

### Visible-detail image

- Show only source-resolved texture, weave, nap, pile, softness cues, thickness, seam, edge, stitching, fringe, glaze, grain, translucency, or finish.
- Do not invent fibers, micro-detail, stitching, hardware, label text, or finish.

### Editorial banner

- Use an approved placement with intentional negative space for live HTML copy.
- Keep text out of the generated image unless explicitly requested.
- Keep product category, use, and exact inventory unambiguous.

## Series planning

- Generate one image per image-generation call, always with the original prototype set.
- Balance product-truth, lifestyle-use, and visible-detail views.
- Keep color, pattern, material, construction, proportions, and behavior stable across the series.
- Let only pose-dependent folds and approved context vary.
- Across the series, show at least one verifiable view of every identity-defining component edge, closure, reversible side, and landmark visible in the prototypes.
- Vary architecture, support or seating type, camera angle, framing, and visual rhythm rather than changing decorations around the same composition.
- Do not repeat the same architecture, seating type, camera angle, or rhythm unless requested.
- Maintain one controlled color-management workflow and a coherent lens/lighting family while allowing genuine editorial variety.
- Keep every frame premium, decorated, and commerce-usable rather than merely natural.
